This study aimed to determine if prophylactic antibiotics could reduce the incidence of endomyometritis after forceps delivery. The trial involved 393 patients, with 192 receiving 2gm of intravenous cefotetan after delivery and 201 receiving no antibiotics. The results showed that there were seven cases of endomyometritis in the group without antibiotics and none in the cefotetan group, a statistically significant difference (P less than .01). The study aimed to investigate the impact of non-scrubbed operating room staff not wearing surgical face masks on surgical site infections (SSIs). A total of 811 patients undergoing various surgeries were enrolled and randomly assigned to either a 'Mask group' or 'No Mask group'. The primary end point, SSI, was identified through in-patient surveillance, post-discharge follow-up, and chart reviews. The results showed that 10.2% of patients in the Masked group and 9.0% in the No Mask group developed SSIs. The odds ratio for SSI in the No Mask group was 0.77 (95% CI 0.49-1.21), which was not statistically significant (p = 0.151). The study concluded that the use of face masks by non-scrubbed operating room personnel did not decrease the risk of SSIs.

In a randomized, double-blind trial, 39 patients with primary sclerosing cholangitis received penicillamine (250 mg t.i.d.), and 31 received a placebo. Both groups were similar at entry. Penicillamine led to expected side effects, including cupruresis and decreased hepatic copper levels. However, there was no significant difference in disease progression or overall survival between the two groups. Progressive symptoms, worsening liver function tests, and histologic progression occurred in over 80% of the patients. Penicillamine was permanently discontinued due to side effects in 21% of the patients taking the drug. The study suggests that primary sclerosing cholangitis is a progressive disease in many patients, and penicillamine does not offer a beneficial effect.
